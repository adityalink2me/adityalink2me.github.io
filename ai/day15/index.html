AI does not replace judgment. It speeds up the questions.

4Visualize

Types of Data Visualizations
Comparison

Bar, column, grouped bar. Best for comparing categories like departments, products, regions, or classes.

Trend over time

Line, area, sparkline. Best for months, weeks, dates, growth, decline, and seasonality.

Part-to-whole

Stacked bar, 100% stacked bar, treemap, donut. Best for share, composition, and mix.

Relationship

Scatter plot, bubble chart. Best for asking whether two numbers move together.

Distribution

Histogram, box plot, frequency table. Best for spread, typical values, and unusual values.

Location

Map chart. Best when geography is central to the question and location names are clean.

Before Charts: Make Data Chartable

Clean data shape

  1. One row equals one record.
  2. One column equals one variable.
  3. Headers are clear and unique.
  4. Dates are actual dates.
  5. Numbers are numbers, not text.
  6. Categories use consistent spelling.
Cleaning audit promptI have a Google Sheets dataset with these columns: [PASTE COLUMN NAMES] Act as a careful data analyst. Tell me: 1. Which columns are dimensions and which are metrics. 2. What data quality problems I should check. 3. Which columns may need standard spelling or formatting. 4. What summary tables and charts are possible. 5. What questions this dataset can and cannot answer. Do not invent values. Ask me for sample rows if needed.
Most chart problems begin as data-shape problemsSlide 6

Use AI to Interpret, Not Invent
Insight explanation promptI created these summary results in Google Sheets: [PASTE SUMMARY TABLE] Act as a cautious data analyst. Write: 1. Three insights supported by the numbers. 2. One possible outlier or unusual pattern. 3. Two follow-up questions we should ask. 4. One limitation of this dataset. 5. A plain-language recommendation for [AUDIENCE]. Rules: - Use only the numbers I provide. - Do not invent causes. - If a relationship is only correlation, say so. - Keep the explanation beginner-friendly.

Good AI use

  • Ask for patterns.
  • Ask for plain-language explanations.
  • Ask for chart suggestions.
  • Ask for limitations.
  • Verify every number in the sheet.
Insight without verification is just confident storytellingSlide 13

Chart Design Rules
Sort intentionally

Sort bars descending for ranking, or use natural order for time and stages.

Reduce clutter

Remove heavy borders, unnecessary legends, and decorative 3D effects.

Use color meaningfully

Use one highlight color to direct attention; avoid rainbow charts.

Label clearly

Use readable axis labels, units, and direct labels when useful.

Show context

Include comparison, baseline, date range, or sample size when needed.

Be honest

Do not crop axes or choose chart types that exaggerate small differences.

Good design makes the truth easier to seeSlide 14
Use one messy chart and fix it live if time allows.
Repair Bad Charts Fast
Chart repair promptAudit this chart description: [DESCRIBE OR PASTE THE CHART DETAILS] Audience: [AUDIENCE] Purpose: [DECISION OR QUESTION] Tell me: 1. Whether this is the right chart type. 2. What should be sorted, filtered, or grouped. 3. What title would make the insight clearer. 4. What labels, units, or source notes are missing. 5. What visual clutter should be removed. 6. Whether the chart could mislead viewers. Give a repaired chart plan for Google Sheets.

Common repairs

  • Replace pie with sorted bar.
  • Use line chart for dates.
  • Move text into the title.
  • Show percentages and counts.
  • Combine tiny categories as Other.
  • Add source and date range.

Data Safety with Chat Tools
Do not paste private data

Avoid names, phone numbers, addresses, IDs, grades, health details, salary, or sensitive business data.

Use samples

Paste column names, fake rows, or aggregated summary tables instead of raw private records.

Mask details

Replace names with Person A, Person B; replace exact locations with regions.

Verify formulas

AI may suggest wrong formulas. Test on a small sample before trusting results.

Keep sources

Record where the data came from, date range, and what was excluded.

Be humble

Charts show patterns. They do not automatically explain causes.

Protect people first; analyze secondSlide 17
This is essential for students handling school, customer, or organizational data.
