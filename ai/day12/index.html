Day 12 | Presentations with AI

From Blank Page to Clear Story.

Today we will use AI as a presentation team: strategist, writer, designer, editor, and rehearsal coach. The goal is not more slides. The goal is a message people can understand and remember.

Presentation Thinking

A presentation is a guided decision, not a decorated document.

Weak starting point

"I need 12 slides about our project."

Better starting point

"After 8 minutes, this audience should understand the problem and approve the next step."

AI's role

Explore structure, draft alternatives, generate visuals, and speed up revisions.

Your role

Choose the truth, argument, examples, tone, and final call to action.

First question: What should the audience think, feel, or do differently?

Purpose controls every slide choiceSlide 2
Have learners rewrite a topic into an audience outcome.
Meet the Presentation Tool Families

Different tools solve different parts of the work.

Thinking partners

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini: clarify audience, story, slide copy, and speaker notes.

AI deck generators

Gamma, Canva, Pitch, Slidesgo, Prezi: turn prompts or outlines into designed drafts.

Source-grounded tools

NotebookLM: create a deck from selected sources and visible evidence.

Traditional editors + AI

PowerPoint Copilot and Gemini in Slides: build inside familiar slide editors.

Open-source generators

Presenton: generate editable PPTX/PDF with more privacy and technical control.

Most reliable workflow: plan in one tool, build in another, review yourself

The Repeatable Workflow

Strong decks are made through a sequence, not one magical prompt.

01Define

Audience, purpose, decision, time.

02Gather

Facts, examples, sources, brand assets.

03Outline

One-sentence story and slide claims.

04Draft

Generate slide content and notes.

05Design

Choose layouts, visuals, and hierarchy.

06Verify

Check facts, sources, readability, access.

07Rehearse

Time the talk and improve transitions.

AI can assist at every step, but skipping a step usually creates a polished-looking weak presentation.

Define - Gather - Outline - Draft - Design - Verify - RehearseSlide 7

Step 1 | Define the Brief

Give AI the presentation behind the presentation.

Audience

Who are they and what do they know?

Outcome

What should change after the talk?

Evidence

What facts or examples must appear?

Constraints

Time, slide count, tone, format.

Voice

What should sound like you?

One-minute brief

Audience: [WHO] Purpose: [INFORM / PERSUADE / TEACH] Afterward they should: [ACTION OR BELIEF] Time: [MINUTES] Must include: [FACTS / EXAMPLES] Avoid: [JARGON / CLAIMS / TOPICS] Final format: [PPTX / PDF / LINK]
A clear brief prevents generic slidesSlide 8

Prompt Recipe | Plan Before Designing
Use in ChatGPT, Claude, or GeminiAct as a presentation strategist. Help me plan a [LENGTH]-minute presentation for [AUDIENCE] about [TOPIC]. The audience currently believes or knows: [CONTEXT]. After the presentation, I want them to: [OUTCOME]. Use only these facts and sources: [PASTE OR ATTACH]. Tone: [TONE]. Constraints: [SLIDE COUNT / FORMAT / BRAND / ACCESSIBILITY]. First, propose a one-sentence thesis and a logical story arc. Then create a slide-by-slide plan. For every slide include: 1. a claim-style title, 2. the single idea it must prove, 3. the best evidence or example, 4. a suggested visual, 5. brief speaker notes, 6. anything that needs verification. Do not invent facts. Identify weak or missing evidence before drafting.

Why this works

  • Separates thinking from decoration.
  • Makes every slide earn its place.
  • Requests visuals and notes together.
  • Surfaces missing evidence early.
  • Creates a useful handoff to any slide generator.
Use a chatbot to improve the story before generating the deckSlide 10
Demonstrate one weak prompt first, then this structured prompt. Compare the outlines.
ChatGPT | Think, Draft, Critique
Outline
Create three alternative story arcs for this presentation: problem-solution, before-after, and question-answer. Recommend the strongest for my audience and explain why.
Slide copy
Rewrite this slide so it has one claim-style title, no more than 35 body words, one concrete example, and a clear visual suggestion. Preserve the factual meaning.
Red-team review
Critique this deck as a skeptical audience member. List the five biggest clarity, evidence, logic, and credibility problems. Then propose focused revisions without adding unsupported facts.

Claude | Long Context, Voice, and Files

Use Claude when the presentation must understand substantial source material.

Source synthesis

Upload approved material and ask for agreements, conflicts, gaps, and a source-based narrative.

Voice preservation

Provide a writing sample and request slide text and notes that preserve your natural tone.

File creation

On supported plans, request a PowerPoint deck; Claude Design may create visual slide work where available.

Claude promptRead the attached sources and build a concise presentation plan for [AUDIENCE]. Use XML-like sections in your response: <thesis>, <story_arc>, <slides>, <sources_by_slide>, and <open_questions>. For each slide, distinguish source-backed statements from interpretation. Keep my voice: [DESCRIBE OR ATTACH SAMPLE]. If your account supports file creation, create an editable PowerPoint after I approve the outline. Otherwise, return a structured handoff for a slide generator.

Gamma | Fast Prompt-to-Deck Draft
Gamma creation promptCreate an 8-card presentation for [AUDIENCE] titled "[TITLE]." Purpose: [OUTCOME]. Story arc: [PASTE APPROVED OUTLINE]. Use only these facts: [FACTS / SOURCES]. Visual direction: clean, confident, high contrast, minimal text, varied layouts, and meaningful diagrams instead of decorative images. Each card must have one claim-style headline, one proof object, and brief supporting text. End with a specific next step. Do not invent statistics or quotations.

Strong Gamma workflow

  1. Plan the outline first.
  2. Generate from the approved outline.
  3. Choose a restrained theme.
  4. Replace generic images.
  5. Shorten dense cards.
  6. Export and inspect PPTX/PDF.
Free plan: starter AI credits and up to 10 cards per promptSlide 14
Show that Gamma cards are web-native and may feel different from fixed PowerPoint slides.

Step 3 | Design a Visual System
One dominant idea

The audience should know where to look first.

Large readable type

Slides support the speaker; they are not pages of a report.

Meaningful visual

Use a chart, process, example, or comparison that proves the title.

Consistent rules

Repeat fonts, colors, spacing, and footer behavior.

Varied layouts

Keep the system consistent without making every slide identical.

Consistency builds trust; variety maintains attentionSlide 18
Ask learners to identify the first, second, and third thing their eye notices.
Prompt Recipe | Make the Design Useful
Design direction promptRevise the visual direction of this presentation for [AUDIENCE AND SETTING]. Use a restrained visual system: - background: [LIGHT / DARK], - typography: large, readable, and professional, - palette: one primary accent plus one support color, - layout: generous whitespace and varied compositions, - visuals: diagrams, comparisons, charts, timelines, and authentic images only when they support the claim, - accessibility: strong contrast, no meaning communicated by color alone, and readable labels. Remove decorative clutter, repeated card grids, generic stock images, and paragraphs. Preserve all verified facts. Explain the design rule for each slide before applying it.

Do not ask only:

"Make it look professional."

Professional means different things for a classroom lesson, investor pitch, policy briefing, community proposal, and sales demo.

Describe the communication job, not just the aestheticSlide 19
Encourage learners to name what the visual design should help the audience do.

Step 5 | Verify Before You Present

Visual confidence can hide factual weakness.

Facts

Are numbers, names, dates, and quotations correct?

Sources

Does each important claim have real supporting evidence?

Logic

Does the evidence actually prove the slide title?

Visuals

Are charts honest, labels readable, and images appropriate?

Access

Can people read, hear, navigate, and understand it?

Verification promptAudit this presentation. Make a table with every factual claim, its slide number, the evidence provided, confidence level, and required verification action. Flag unsupported statistics, quotations, dates, causal claims, and images that may misrepresent the topic. Do not silently repair facts.

Human checks still required

  • Open original sources.
  • Inspect exports slide by slide.
  • Confirm rights and privacy.
  • Test links, video, and fonts.
  • Ask a real person what is unclear.
Presentation-ready means evidence-readySlide 21
Use a sample AI-generated statistic and show how easily it can look trustworthy on a slide.
Step 6 | Use AI as a Rehearsal Coach
Time it
Turn these speaker notes into a timed run-of-show for an 8-minute presentation. Mark slides that are likely to run long and propose cuts.
Prepare questions
Act as a skeptical member of [AUDIENCE]. Ask 12 likely questions, including three difficult objections. Draft concise evidence-based answers and flag where I lack evidence.
Simplify delivery
Rewrite these notes for natural spoken delivery. Shorten long sentences, explain jargon, preserve my meaning, and add clear transitions. Do not make the tone theatrical.

Final rehearsal rule: present aloud to a person, not only to AI.

Rehearsal reveals problems the editor cannotSlide 22
Pair learners. One presents for 60 seconds; the other notes the first confusing moment.

Hands-on 1 | Plan the Presentation

Create the strategic brief

  1. Choose a real topic.
  2. Define audience and desired outcome.
  3. Gather only safe, relevant source material.
  4. Use the master planning prompt.
  5. Approve or revise the thesis and story arc.
Time: 25 minutes

Visible output

Brief

Audience, outcome, constraints.

Thesis

One sentence the deck proves.

Outline

Evidence gaps

What still needs checking.

Hands-on 3 | Human Editing Sprint
Delete

Remove slides that repeat, distract, or fail to advance the story.

Sharpen

Turn every title into the message the audience should remember.

Prove

Add a source, example, diagram, or comparison that supports each claim.

Simplify

Reduce text and explain jargon in plain language.

Personalize

Add your voice, local context, authentic examples, and real next step.

Focused revision promptRevise this deck while preserving the approved thesis and verified facts. Remove repetition, rewrite topic titles as claims, reduce visible text, replace generic visuals with meaningful proof objects, and strengthen the final call to action. List every factual change separately for human approval.
Generation creates material; editing creates the presentationSlide 26
This is the most important production block. Participants should spend more time editing than generating.
Hands-on 4 | Silent Audience Test

Review without explanation

  1. Give the deck to a partner.
  2. Do not explain the topic.
  3. Partner writes the main message.
  4. Partner marks the first confusing slide.
  5. Partner names one missing question.
  6. Creator makes one focused revision.
Time: 20 minutes

Score the deck from 1-5

Clear purpose and audience___
Logical story___
Readable slides___
Credible evidence___
Specific next step___
Sounds like the presenter___
If a silent reader misses the message, the deck needs repairSlide 27
