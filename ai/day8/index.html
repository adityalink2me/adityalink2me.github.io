The Problem

Collecting documents is easy. Building understanding is the hard part.

Too much material

Reports, PDFs, videos, notes, slides, and websites pile up faster than we can read.

Facts are scattered

One answer may require connections across several sources.

Memory fades

Passive rereading feels productive but rarely proves mastery.

Generic AI guesses

A chatbot may answer beyond your trusted evidence or invent missing detail.

NotebookLM changes the starting question: not "What does AI know?" but "What can we understand from these sources?"

Control the evidence before generating the answerSlide 2

Why It Is Different

NotebookLM is strongest when the answer must stay close to selected evidence.

It uses the sources in the current notebook as its knowledge base. Answers include inline citations that let you jump back to the supporting passage.

Evidence boundarySource subset selectionCitation tracingCross-source synthesis

Grounded does not mean guaranteed

  • A citation may not fully support the claim.
  • A weak or biased source remains weak or biased.
  • Missing information can produce incomplete conclusions.
  • AI can misunderstand complicated tables or context.
  • You still need to open citations and verify important claims.
Grounding reduces wandering; verification remains your jobSlide 4
Do not describe NotebookLM as perfectly accurate. Demonstrate opening one citation and checking the original context.

Curate Before You Create

A focused source pack produces a more useful notebook.

1Define the decision

What must the notebook help you understand, teach, compare, or decide?

2Prefer originals

Use policies, research papers, official guidance, and firsthand records.

3Add perspectives

Include sources that disagree or serve different audiences.

4Name clearly

Use titles that reveal date, owner, subject, and version.

5Remove noise

Exclude duplicates, obsolete drafts, and irrelevant material.

Source audit promptReview the selected sources. Create a table with source purpose, author or owner, date, likely strengths, likely limitations, and what important perspective is missing.
Better sources beat more sourcesSlide 6

Grounded Chat

Good questions move from orientation to challenge to creation.

1Orient

What are the major themes and terms?

2Locate

Where is a specific rule, claim, or example?

3Connect

How do sources support or contradict each other?

4Challenge

What is weak, missing, uncertain, or biased?

5Translate

Explain for a particular learner or audience.

6Create

Transform the understanding into a useful output.

Strong chat promptUsing only the selected sources, explain the three most important agreements and two meaningful disagreements. Cite every point, distinguish evidence from interpretation, and tell me which conclusion is least certain.
Select all sources or a focused subset before askingSlide 8
Show that source selection changes the answer. Ask the same question with one source selected and then with all sources selected.

What should you do after receiving a cited answer?

The TRACE check

  • Text: does the passage say this?
  • Relevance: is it evidence for this exact claim?
  • Authority: who created the source?
  • Currency: is the date appropriate?
  • Exceptions: what has been omitted?
Citations make verification faster, not optionalSlide 9
Use a real citation from the class notebook and apply TRACE together.

Notes Become Thinking Material
Write your own notes

Capture interpretations, questions, examples, and decisions alongside the sources.

Save strong answers

Turn a useful chat response into a note. Saved response notes are not editable.

Transform notes

Combine, summarize, critique, outline, create a study guide, or convert notes into sources.

Collaboration and export

Written notes sync for notebook editors. Notes can be exported to Google Docs, while data tables can be exported to Google Sheets.

Current capacity

Google currently documents up to 1,000 notes per notebook. Treat notes as your reasoning layer, not a storage dump.

Mind Maps

Use a mind map to see the structure before studying the details.

Mind Maps visually summarize sources as a branching diagram of main topics and connected ideas. They are useful for orientation, exploring relationships, and organizing memory.

Follow-up learning moveChoose one branch that feels unclear. Ask chat to explain the branch, compare it with another branch, cite the relevant sources, and give one real-world example.

Best for

  • Seeing the big picture
  • Finding a starting point
  • Connecting concepts
  • Planning an explanation
  • Identifying missing branches
Not currently supported in the mobile app
Map first, then question the branchesSlide 15
A map shows a proposed structure. Learners should challenge whether the hierarchy is meaningful and complete.

Practice for Mastery

Flashcards and quizzes turn passive reading into active recall.

Customize

Choose easy, medium, or hard and describe audience, style, and focus.

Explain

Ask for explanations when a card or answer is unclear.

Track

Mark flashcards as "Got it" or "Missed it" and return later.

Review

See results, revisit errors, and retake the activity.

Better practice prompt

Create hard application questions for a new manager. Focus on choosing the correct action in realistic scenarios, not memorizing definitions. Explain why each wrong answer is tempting.
Mastery requires retrieval, explanation, application, and correctionSlide 17
Ask learners to generate a quiz, answer without looking at sources, and then verify one explanation.
A Complete Learning Loop

Do not stop at a summary. Make the notebook teach, test, and improve you.

1Preview

Use summary, audio, or mind map.

2Question

Ask what is important and confusing.

3Explain

Restate ideas in your own words.

4Retrieve

Use flashcards and quizzes.

5Apply

Solve a realistic case or decision.

6Verify

Open citations and repair mistakes.

Learning coach promptTeach me this topic in three rounds. First diagnose what I know. Then explain only what I am missing using cited examples. Finally test me with one recall question, one comparison question, and one realistic application case.
Understanding grows through effort, feedback, and correctionSlide 18

Share and Collaborate Carefully
Private notebook

A lock icon indicates the notebook has not been shared. Keep sensitive or unfinished work private.

Shared notebook

Add collaborators as viewers or editors where account and organization settings allow it.

Public notebook

Consumer accounts can share a link with anyone who has a Google account; public sharing is disabled for Enterprise and Education accounts.

Chat View is not an access-control barrier. It gives a focused view, but viewers may still be able to navigate to underlying sources and artifacts. Share only content you are allowed to expose.

Privacy and Responsible Use

Source control includes permission, privacy, and data handling.

Permission

Only upload material you are allowed to use and process.

Minimize

Remove personal, confidential, medical, financial, and identifying data.

Feedback caution

Providing feedback may share the interaction context for review.

Account rules

Work and school accounts follow their applicable Google terms and controls.

Google's current statement

Notebook content is not used to directly train foundational AI models unless the user chooses to provide feedback. Other connected Google services follow their own notices and privacy terms.

Always follow your organization's policy
Safe practice: use the least sensitive source pack that can do the jobSlide 22
